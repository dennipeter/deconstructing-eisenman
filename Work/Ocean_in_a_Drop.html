Ocean in a Drop

Kinetic / Interactive
Collaborators
Aditi Anuj,
Sapna Khatri
"Ocean in a Drop" is a kinetic art installation that was developed in collaboration with Aditi Anuj. The piece was displayed at the Delhi Contemporary Art Week 2022 in Bikaner House. The installation draws inspiration from a quote by Rumi - "You're not a drop in the ocean. You're an entire ocean in a drop."
This dynamic installation made its debut at the "Delhi Contemporary Art Week 2023" in collaboration with Aditi Anuj, who is an origami artist based in Jaipur. Aditi's exhibition centered around the theme of water, where she envisioned a creation that would capture the essence of the ocean's fluctuating motions. 27 individual modules made of pleated origami folds take on the metaphor for a drop of water. These drops combine to form an oceanic landscape that sways in harmony with the motion of waves.

Adorned with beautiful watercolour details painted by Sapna Khatri, the installation's bi-chromatic blue hues complement the interplay of the dark and light shades found within the ocean. Quoting Aditi - "The fleeting nature of the wave represents the unfolding of life and everything else it brings along."
Design & Visual Style
The instalation in motion
Aditi's work is a testament to her skillful manipulation of form and geometry. Her artistic vision manifests in the form of intricate designs, which she brings to life through her exploration of pleated folds and the use of modular elements. The design process began with an extensive examination of her previous works, as we sought ways to add fluidity and motion to her pieces. Ultimately, we landed on the V-pleated fold, which proved to be mechanically compliant and aesthetically pleasing. With its myriad facets and varying angles, this fold structure bears a striking resemblance to the folds of an accordion.

The V-pleated fold's unique contours offer ample surface area for intricate painting work, and its ability to virtually disappear when completely collapsed imbues the piece with great dynamics(both in terms of movement as well as its visual presentation). By combining precise geometric forms with kinetic elements, we wanted to create a piece that is at once mesmerizing, intriguing, and endlessly fascinating for the viewer.
Various animation sequences
From the outset, it was evident that multiple motors would be necessary to control similar modules made up of Aditi's intricate pleated folds. Thus, the mechanical design process began from scratch, aiming to animate these pleats in the most straightforward manner. Initially, we explored linear actuator mechanisms to animate the pleats linearly. However, we eventually decided to fold the pleats around a semi-circular path, which was not only simpler to implement mechanically but also visually more captivating. The resulting curved profile imbued the installation with greater depth, while the movement conveyed a heightened sense of dynamism.

The mesmerizing movement of the installation is made possible by a unique mechanism that utilizes a servo motor with an arm that sweeps 180 degrees. One end of the pleated fold is attached to this moving arm, while the other end is simply fixed along a curved rail. The beauty of this design is in its simplicity - the arm's smooth motion along the semi-circular path is all that's needed to set the entire installation in motion. The intricate pleats and folds themselves, and not the mechanics, are the true stars of the show.
Sapna Khatri's intricate watercolor details
The shade of blue varies when viewed from either end of the installation
Aditi collaborated with Sapna Khatri, a watercolour artist, to create the intricate patterns on the origami. With the V-shaped pleats offering various facets and interesting opportunities, Aditi aimed to capture the complex textures of the ocean, from its turbulent white foam to its calm yet deep blue depths. Sapna's work resulted in a mesmerizing, detailed pattern that features two distinct shades of blue, with the left and right facets painted in light and dark blues respectively. The visual effect of the installation is further enhanced when the pleats are set in motion. When the two opposing facets of the pleats are compressed and come together, they form an ever-changing gradient of blues that is truly captivating to watch.
Construction & Working
The paper was first painted and then folded into the pleated pattern
The origami folds are fully supported and secured by the mechanical framework. The mechanics consist mainly of plastic parts that have been produced through 3D printing and laser-cutting. Positioned at the center of the rotational plane is the servo motor. The motors are accommodated in a 3D-printed bracket that is fixed to a wooden framework. All wiring and electronics are hidden behind this framework. The entire installation consists of 3 such wooden frameworks for easier transportation and installation. Each framework consists of a staggered grid of 3x3 modules. The mechanical parts were all white color to merge into the background, which helps to emphasize the blue of the origami as much as possible.
3D model showing the mechanical and structural framework
The electronic system comprises an ESP-32 microcontroller that serves as an intermediary for managing the servo motors. This controller is linked to a controlling PC via USB serial, where the animation is played. The animation technique utilized in this system is similar to that of 'Bionic Blooms'. Essentially, it transforms greyscale values of pixels into rotational degrees, which regulate the angle of the servo motors. It is also feasible to utilize a Kinect or another similar depth-sensing camera to enable motion tracking and control of the individual motors.
